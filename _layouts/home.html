About me

I study phonology, the linguistic knowledge of sound patterns, and its interaction with other modules such as morphology. The research goal of phonology and its interfaces is to understand the mental representations and computations, as well as the grammatical architecture in the human mind.

In my research, I ask a general question that how some seemingly complex patterns that used to be handled by specific theories, such as phonology-reduplication interaction, can be explained with some simple cognitive principles. In a broad sense, I probe into the question that to what extent phonological knowledge can be economically represented and computed in the human mind. Following my general research question, I investigated the phenomena at the phonology-morphology interface and the phonology-phonetics interface in a diverse range of languages, with my fieldwork data or existing corpora.

My recent research projects feature Huozhou Chinese and Ladin. For Huozhou, I focus on the patterns of diminutive formation and explore how understudied Chinese dialects can inform phonological representation and grammar. For Ladin, my current work involves description and analyses of phonotactics with fieldwork data and acoustic experiments.
